Key Vocabulary

  • Derivative: The rate of change of a function with respect to its variable, representing instantaneous rates in physics.
  • Integral: The accumulation of quantities, used to find areas under curves and total quantities in physics.
  • Vector: A quantity that has both magnitude and direction, represented by arrows in physics diagrams.
  • Scalar: A quantity that has only magnitude, without direction.
  • Dimensional Analysis: A technique for checking the consistency of equations by comparing units.

Unit 2 - Kinematics

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze motion in one and two dimensions using calculus-based methods.
  • Understand the relationships between position, velocity, and acceleration.
  • Solve problems involving projectile motion and relative motion.
  • Use vector notation to describe motion in multiple dimensions.
  • Apply calculus to analyze motion with time-dependent acceleration.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 5 to 6 class periods to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 14% to 20% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • How do position, velocity, and acceleration relate to each other mathematically?
  • What techniques are used to analyze motion in multiple dimensions?
  • How does calculus help solve problems with time-varying acceleration?
  • What are the key differences between 1D and 2D motion analysis?
  • How can we use kinematic equations to predict and describe motion?

Key Concepts

  • Position Vector: A vector that describes the location of an object relative to a reference point.
  • Velocity Vector: The derivative of position with respect to time, describing both speed and direction.
  • Acceleration Vector: The derivative of velocity with respect to time, describing the rate of change of velocity.
  • Projectile Motion: Motion of an object under the influence of gravity alone, analyzed using vector components.
  • Relative Motion: Analysis of motion from different reference frames using vector addition.

Key Vocabulary

  • Displacement: The change in position of an object, represented as a vector quantity.
  • Instantaneous Velocity: The velocity at a specific moment in time, found by taking the derivative of position.
  • Average Acceleration: The change in velocity divided by the time interval over which the change occurs.
  • Trajectory: The path followed by a projectile through space.
  • Reference Frame: A coordinate system used to describe the position and motion of objects.

Unit 3 - Newton's Laws of Motion

Learning Objectives

  • Understand and apply Newton's three laws of motion to various physical situations.
  • Analyze forces acting on objects using free-body diagrams.
  • Solve problems involving friction, tension, and normal forces.
  • Apply Newton's laws to systems of multiple interacting objects.
  • Understand the relationship between force and motion in circular motion.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 5 to 6 class periods to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 17% to 23% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • What do Newton's three laws tell us about the relationship between force and motion?
  • How do we identify and analyze all forces acting on an object?
  • What techniques are used to solve problems with multiple interacting objects?
  • How does friction affect motion, and how can it be quantified?
  • What distinguishes uniform circular motion from other types of motion?

Key Concepts

  • Newton's First Law: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by a net force.
  • Newton's Second Law: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force and inversely proportional to mass (F = ma).
  • Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.
  • Free-Body Diagrams: Visual representations showing all forces acting on an object.
  • Circular Motion: Motion in a circular path requiring centripetal force directed toward the center.

Key Vocabulary

  • Inertia: The tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion.
  • Net Force: The vector sum of all forces acting on an object.
  • Normal Force: The contact force exerted by a surface on an object in contact with it.
  • Friction: The force that opposes the relative motion or tendency of motion between surfaces in contact.
  • Centripetal Force: The inward force required to keep an object moving in a circular path.

Unit 4 - Work, Energy, and Power

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the concepts of work, energy, and power and their relationships.
  • Apply the work-energy theorem to solve problems involving kinetic energy.
  • Analyze conservative and non-conservative forces and their effects on energy.
  • Use conservation of energy principles to solve complex mechanical problems.
  • Calculate power and understand its relationship to work and time.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 5 to 6 class periods to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 14% to 20% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • What is the relationship between work, energy, and power in physical systems?
  • How does the work-energy theorem simplify problem-solving in mechanics?
  • What distinguishes conservative forces from non-conservative forces?
  • How is energy conserved in isolated systems, and when is it not?
  • What are the practical applications of power calculations in engineering?

Key Concepts

  • Work: The product of force and displacement when force acts in the direction of motion.
  • Kinetic Energy: The energy of motion, proportional to the square of velocity.
  • Potential Energy: Stored energy due to position or configuration in a force field.
  • Conservation of Energy: The total energy in an isolated system remains constant over time.
  • Power: The rate at which work is done or energy is transferred.

Common Errors

  • Confusing Work and Energy: Students often think work and energy are the same thing, but work is the transfer of energy, not energy itself.
  • Sign Errors in Work Calculations: Forgetting that work can be negative when force and displacement are in opposite directions.
  • Misapplying Conservation of Energy: Including non-conservative forces like friction without accounting for energy loss as heat.
  • Power vs. Energy Confusion: Mixing up power (rate of energy transfer) with energy (total amount of work done).
  • Path Independence Misconception: Not understanding that work done by conservative forces is path-independent, while non-conservative forces depend on the path taken.

Unit 5 - Systems of Particles and Linear Momentum

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the concept of center of mass and its motion.
  • Analyze systems of particles and their collective behavior.
  • Apply conservation of linear momentum to solve collision problems.
  • Distinguish between elastic and inelastic collisions.
  • Analyze two-dimensional collision problems using vector methods.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 5 to 6 class periods to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 14% to 17% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • How does the center of mass of a system behave under external forces?
  • What conditions must be met for momentum to be conserved in a system?
  • How do elastic and inelastic collisions differ in terms of energy conservation?
  • What techniques are used to analyze two-dimensional collision problems?
  • How does the concept of impulse relate to changes in momentum?

Key Concepts

  • Center of Mass: The point where the total mass of a system can be considered to be concentrated.
  • Linear Momentum: The product of mass and velocity, conserved in isolated systems.
  • Impulse: The change in momentum resulting from a force applied over time.
  • Elastic Collisions: Collisions where both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.
  • Inelastic Collisions: Collisions where momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not.

Common Errors

  • Confusing Momentum and Energy Conservation: Students often mix up when to use conservation of momentum versus conservation of energy, not realizing momentum is always conserved in isolated systems but kinetic energy is only conserved in elastic collisions.
  • Vector Direction Mistakes: Forgetting that momentum is a vector quantity and not properly accounting for directions in collision problems, especially in 2D collisions.
  • Impulse Direction Errors: Misunderstanding that impulse equals the change in momentum, leading to sign errors when calculating final velocities.
  • Elastic vs. Inelastic Collision Confusion: Not recognizing that inelastic collisions involve energy loss (usually as heat or deformation) while elastic collisions conserve both momentum and kinetic energy.
  • Center of Mass Calculation Errors: Incorrectly calculating center of mass by not properly weighting each mass by its position, or using the wrong coordinate system.

Unit 6 - Rotational Motion

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze rotational dynamics using torque and Newton's second law for rotation.
  • Calculate moments of inertia for various geometric shapes and systems.
  • Apply conservation of angular momentum to rotational systems.
  • Solve problems involving rolling motion and combined translational and rotational motion.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 5 to 7 class periods to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 10% to 17% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • How do angular position, velocity, and acceleration relate to their linear counterparts?
  • What role does torque play in rotational motion, and how is it calculated?
  • How does the moment of inertia affect rotational motion and energy?
  • Under what conditions is angular momentum conserved in rotational systems?
  • How do we analyze objects that exhibit both translational and rotational motion?

Key Concepts

  • Rotational Kinematics: The study of rotational motion using angular position, velocity, and acceleration.
  • Torque: The rotational equivalent of force, causing angular acceleration.
  • Moment of Inertia: The rotational equivalent of mass, depending on mass distribution.
  • Angular Momentum: The rotational equivalent of linear momentum, conserved in isolated systems.
  • Rolling Motion: Combined translational and rotational motion of rigid bodies.

Common Errors

  • Confusing Linear and Rotational Quantities: Students often mix up linear quantities (velocity, acceleration, force) with their rotational counterparts (angular velocity, angular acceleration, torque), leading to incorrect substitutions in equations.
  • Moment of Inertia Calculation Mistakes: Using the wrong moment of inertia formula for different shapes, or forgetting to use the parallel axis theorem when the rotation axis is not through the center of mass.
  • Sign Convention Errors: Inconsistent use of positive and negative signs for angular quantities, especially when dealing with clockwise and counterclockwise rotations.
  • Torque Direction Confusion: Misapplying the right-hand rule to determine torque direction, or confusing torque direction with angular velocity direction.
  • Energy Conservation in Rotation: Forgetting to include rotational kinetic energy in conservation of energy problems, or incorrectly applying the relationship between translational and rotational motion in rolling objects.

Unit 7 - Angular Momentum

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the concept of angular momentum and its conservation.
  • Calculate angular momentum for point particles and rigid bodies.
  • Analyze systems where angular momentum is conserved.
  • Solve problems involving torque and the rate of change of angular momentum.
  • Apply angular momentum principles to real-world rotational systems.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 3 to 5 class periods to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 6% to 14% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • How is angular momentum related to linear momentum and rotational motion?
  • Under what conditions is angular momentum conserved in physical systems?
  • How does the rate of change of angular momentum relate to applied torques?
  • What are the implications of angular momentum conservation in astronomy?
  • How do gyroscopes and spinning objects demonstrate angular momentum principles?

Key Concepts

  • Angular Momentum: The rotational equivalent of linear momentum, conserved in isolated systems.
  • Conservation Law: Angular momentum remains constant in systems with no external torques.
  • Torque-Angular Momentum Relationship: Torque equals the rate of change of angular momentum.
  • Precession: The change in orientation of a rotating object's axis due to external torques.
  • Gyroscopic Effects: The behavior of spinning objects that resist changes to their orientation.

Common Errors

  • Confusing Angular and Linear Momentum: Students often mix up angular momentum (rotational) with linear momentum (translational), not recognizing that they are distinct quantities with different conservation laws and applications.
  • Vector Direction Mistakes: Forgetting that angular momentum is a vector quantity with direction determined by the right-hand rule, leading to errors in problems involving changing angular momentum directions.
  • External Torque Misconceptions: Not understanding when angular momentum is conserved - students often think it's always conserved, but it's only conserved when there's no net external torque.
  • Sign Convention Errors: Inconsistent use of positive and negative signs for angular momentum and torque, especially when dealing with multiple rotating objects or changing rotation directions.
  • Precession Concept Confusion: Misunderstanding precession as a violation of angular momentum conservation, rather than a result of external torques causing the angular momentum vector to change direction while maintaining its magnitude.

  • What is the relationship between kinetic and potential energy in SHM?
  • How does the frequency of oscillation depend on system parameters?

Key Concepts

  • Simple Harmonic Motion: Periodic motion where the restoring force is proportional to displacement.
  • Hooke's Law: The force exerted by a spring is proportional to its displacement from equilibrium.
  • Energy in SHM: The continuous exchange between kinetic and potential energy in oscillating systems.
  • Resonance: The phenomenon where a system oscillates with maximum amplitude at its natural frequency.
  • Damped Oscillations: Oscillations that decrease in amplitude due to dissipative forces.

Key Vocabulary

  • Amplitude: The maximum displacement from equilibrium in an oscillating system.
  • Frequency: The number of oscillations per unit time, measured in Hertz.
  • Period: The time required for one complete oscillation cycle.
  • Phase Constant: A parameter that determines the initial position and velocity of an oscillator.
  • Restoring Force: A force that acts to return a displaced object to its equilibrium position.

Key Vocabulary

  • Gravitational Field: A vector field that describes the gravitational force per unit mass at each point in space.
  • Orbital Velocity: The velocity required for an object to maintain a stable orbit around a massive body.
  • Escape Velocity: The minimum velocity needed for an object to escape the gravitational field of a massive body.
  • Tidal Forces: Differential gravitational forces that arise due to variations in gravitational field strength across an extended object.

Unit 11 - Electrostatics

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the properties of electric charge and Coulomb's law.
  • Analyze electric fields and their effects on charged particles.
  • Calculate electric field due to point charges and continuous charge distributions.
  • Understand electric potential and potential energy in electrostatic systems.
  • Apply Gauss's law to solve problems involving symmetric charge distributions.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 20 class periods to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 20% to 26% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • What are the fundamental properties of electric charge and how do charged objects interact?
  • How do electric fields represent the influence of charged objects on their surroundings?
  • What is the relationship between electric field and electric potential?
  • How can Gauss's law simplify calculations for symmetric charge distributions?
  • What distinguishes conductors from insulators in electrostatic situations?

Key Concepts

  • Electric Charge: Fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience electromagnetic forces.
  • Coulomb's Law: The force between two point charges is proportional to the product of charges and inversely proportional to the square of distance.
  • Electric Field: A vector field that describes the force per unit charge at each point in space.
  • Electric Potential: The potential energy per unit charge at a point in an electric field.
  • Gauss's Law: The electric flux through any closed surface is proportional to the enclosed charge.

Common Errors

  • Confusing Electric Field and Electric Force: Students often mix up electric field (force per unit charge) with electric force, forgetting that E = F/q, so the electric field is independent of the test charge.
  • Sign Errors with Electric Charges: Forgetting that electric field direction depends on the sign of the source charge - field lines point away from positive charges and toward negative charges.
  • Vector Addition Mistakes: Incorrectly adding electric field vectors by treating them as scalars instead of vectors, especially when dealing with multiple charges.
  • Gauss's Law Misapplication: Using Gauss's law for non-symmetric charge distributions where it doesn't simplify calculations, or choosing incorrect Gaussian surfaces.
  • Electric Potential vs. Electric Field Confusion: Not understanding that electric potential is a scalar quantity while electric field is a vector, and confusing their relationships and units.
  • Superposition Principle Errors: Forgetting to apply the superposition principle correctly when calculating net electric fields or potentials from multiple sources.

Unit 12 - Conductors, Capacitors, Dielectrics

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the behavior of conductors in electrostatic equilibrium and the resulting electric field and charge distribution.
  • Analyze the principles of capacitance and calculate capacitance for various geometric configurations.
  • Understand the effect of dielectrics on capacitance and electric fields in capacitors.
  • Calculate energy stored in capacitors and analyze RC circuits.
  • Solve problems involving combinations of capacitors in series and parallel.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 20 class periods to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 10% to 17% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • How do conductors behave when placed in external electric fields, and what are the resulting charge distributions?
  • What factors determine the capacitance of different geometric configurations like parallel plates, cylinders, and spheres?
  • How do dielectric materials affect the capacitance and electric field within capacitors?
  • What is the relationship between charge, voltage, and energy storage in capacitors?
  • How do capacitors behave when connected in series and parallel combinations?

Key Concepts

  • Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium: Electric field inside a conductor is zero, and excess charge resides on the surface.
  • Capacitance: The ability of a system to store electric charge per unit voltage, C = Q/V.
  • Dielectrics: Insulating materials that increase capacitance when placed between capacitor plates.
  • Energy Storage: Capacitors store energy in the electric field between their plates, U = ½CV².
  • Capacitor Combinations: Series and parallel combinations follow specific rules for equivalent capacitance.

Common Errors

  • Conductor Equilibrium Misconceptions: Students often think that electric fields can exist inside conductors in electrostatic equilibrium, not understanding that the electric field inside a conductor must be zero, and all excess charge resides on the surface.
  • Capacitance Formula Confusion: Mixing up the formulas for different capacitor geometries (parallel plate, cylindrical, spherical) or using incorrect formulas, leading to calculation errors in capacitance problems.
  • Series vs. Parallel Capacitor Errors: Confusing the rules for series and parallel capacitor combinations - students often reverse the addition rules, thinking capacitors in series add like resistors in series.
  • Dielectric Effect Misunderstanding: Not understanding that dielectrics increase capacitance by reducing the electric field between plates, or confusing dielectric constant with other material properties.
  • Energy Storage Calculation Mistakes: Using the wrong formula for energy stored in capacitors (U = ½CV², ½Q²/C, or ½QV) or mixing up which variables are known in a given problem.
  • Charge Distribution Errors: Incorrectly assuming that charge is distributed evenly in series capacitor combinations, when in fact capacitors in series have the same charge but different voltages.

Unit 14 - Electric Circuits

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the fundamental principles of electric current, voltage, and resistance in DC circuits.
  • Analyze series and parallel circuits using Ohm's law and Kirchhoff's laws.
  • Calculate equivalent resistance and apply circuit analysis techniques to complex networks.
  • Understand the behavior of RC circuits including charging and discharging processes.
  • Analyze power dissipation and energy transfer in electrical circuits.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 15 class periods to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 10% to 17% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • What are the fundamental relationships between current, voltage, and resistance in electrical circuits?
  • How do series and parallel circuit configurations affect the behavior of electrical components?
  • What techniques are used to analyze complex circuits with multiple resistors and power sources?
  • How do capacitors behave in DC circuits during charging and discharging processes?
  • What are the principles of power dissipation and energy transfer in electrical systems?

Key Concepts

  • Ohm's Law: The relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in electrical circuits (V = IR).
  • Kirchhoff's Laws: Conservation laws for charge (junction rule) and energy (loop rule) in electrical circuits.
  • Series and Parallel Circuits: Different circuit configurations with distinct current and voltage relationships.
  • RC Circuits: Circuits containing resistors and capacitors, exhibiting time-dependent behavior.
  • Power and Energy: The rate of energy transfer and total energy dissipated in electrical circuits.

Common Errors

  • Ohm's Law Misapplication: Students often apply Ohm's law incorrectly by using the wrong voltage across a resistor or confusing total circuit voltage with individual component voltages.
  • Series vs. Parallel Confusion: Mixing up the rules for series (same current, voltages add) and parallel (same voltage, currents add) circuits, leading to calculation errors.
  • Kirchhoff's Laws Errors: Incorrectly applying junction and loop rules, especially with sign conventions for voltage drops and current directions.
  • Equivalent Resistance Mistakes: Using the wrong formulas for combining resistors in series and parallel, or failing to simplify complex networks step by step.
  • RC Circuit Time Constant Errors: Confusing the charging and discharging equations for RC circuits, or using incorrect time constant values (τ = RC).
  • Power Calculation Confusion: Using the wrong power formula (P = IV, P = I²R, or P = V²/R) or mixing up which variables apply to specific components.

Unit 15 - Magnetic Fields

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the properties of magnetic fields and their sources including permanent magnets and current-carrying conductors.
  • Analyze the forces experienced by charged particles and current-carrying conductors in magnetic fields.
  • Calculate magnetic fields due to simple current distributions using the Biot-Savart law and Ampère's law.
  • Understand the relationship between electric and magnetic fields in electromagnetic induction.
  • Analyze the behavior of charged particles in combined electric and magnetic fields.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 12 class periods to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 10% to 17% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • What are the fundamental properties of magnetic fields and how do they differ from electric fields?
  • How do moving charges and current-carrying conductors create magnetic fields?
  • What forces do charged particles experience when moving through magnetic fields?
  • How can we calculate magnetic fields for symmetric current distributions?
  • What is the relationship between electricity and magnetism in electromagnetic phenomena?

Key Concepts

  • Magnetic Field: A vector field that describes the magnetic influence on moving electric charges and magnetic materials.
  • Lorentz Force: The force experienced by a charged particle moving through electric and magnetic fields.
  • Biot-Savart Law: The law that describes the magnetic field due to a small current element.
  • Ampère's Law: The relationship between magnetic field and the current that produces it.
  • Magnetic Force on Currents: The force experienced by current-carrying conductors in magnetic fields.

Common Errors

  • Confusing Electric and Magnetic Fields: Students often mix up electric fields (caused by static charges) with magnetic fields (caused by moving charges or currents), not understanding their different properties and behaviors.
  • Right-Hand Rule Mistakes: Incorrect application of right-hand rules for determining magnetic field directions and forces, leading to sign errors in force and field calculations.
  • Lorentz Force Direction Errors: Forgetting that magnetic force is always perpendicular to both velocity and magnetic field, or using the wrong hand for negative charges.
  • Ampère's Law Misapplication: Using Ampère's law for non-symmetric current distributions where it doesn't simplify calculations, or choosing incorrect Amperian loops.
  • Vector Cross Product Confusion: Not understanding that magnetic force involves vector cross products, leading to incorrect force directions and magnitudes.
  • Units and Constants Errors: Mixing up magnetic field units (Tesla vs. Gauss) or using incorrect values for magnetic constants in calculations.

Unit 16 - Electromagnetism

Learning Objectives

  • Understand Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction and Lenz's law.
  • Analyze the induced emf and current in conducting loops due to changing magnetic flux.
  • Calculate induced electric fields due to time-varying magnetic fields.
  • Understand the operation of inductors and RL circuits.
  • Analyze Maxwell's equations qualitatively and their implications for electromagnetic waves.

Curriculum Standards

  • The unit will take approximately 2 weeks to complete.
  • This part of the curriculum contributes to a 10% to 17% weight in the AP exam.

Guiding Questions

  • How does a changing magnetic field induce an electric field and current in a conductor?
  • What determines the magnitude and direction of induced emf and current?
  • How do inductors store energy in magnetic fields and affect circuit behavior?
  • What are the fundamental relationships between electric and magnetic fields described by Maxwell's equations?
  • How do electromagnetic waves propagate through space?

Key Concepts

  • Faraday's Law: The induced emf in a closed loop equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through the loop.
  • Lenz's Law: The direction of induced current opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it.
  • Magnetic Flux: The product of magnetic field, area, and the cosine of the angle between them.
  • Inductance: The property of a circuit element that opposes changes in current, storing energy in magnetic fields.
  • Maxwell's Equations: Four fundamental equations describing the behavior of electric and magnetic fields and their interactions.

Common Errors

  • Flux Calculation Mistakes: Students often forget to consider the angle between the magnetic field and the surface normal when calculating magnetic flux, or confuse the area vector direction.
  • Lenz's Law Direction Errors: Incorrectly determining the direction of induced current by not properly applying Lenz's law to oppose the change in flux, often getting the direction backwards.
  • Faraday's Law Misapplication: Forgetting that Faraday's law involves the rate of change of flux, not just the flux itself, or using the wrong sign convention.
  • Inductor Behavior Confusion: Not understanding that inductors oppose changes in current, leading to errors in analyzing RL circuit behavior during switching events.
  • Maxwell's Equations Misconceptions: Confusing the different forms of Maxwell's equations or not understanding their physical significance and applications.
  • Units and Constants Errors: Mixing up magnetic flux units (Weber) or using incorrect values for electromagnetic constants in calculations.
