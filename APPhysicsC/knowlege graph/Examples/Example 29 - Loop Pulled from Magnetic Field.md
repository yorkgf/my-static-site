# Example 29: Force to Pull a Loop from a Magnetic Field

## Problem Statement

Part of a single rectangular loop of wire with dimensions shown is situated inside a region of uniform magnetic field of 0.650 T. The total resistance of the loop is 0.250 Ω. Calculate the force required to pull the loop from the field (to the right) at a constant velocity of 3.40 m/s. Neglect gravity.

## Solution

### Step 1: EMF from Motional Induction

As the loop is pulled to the right at speed $v$, the left edge of the field boundary sweeps through the loop. The motional EMF is generated by the segment of wire crossing the field boundary (vertical wire of height $\ell = 0.350$ m moving at speed $v$):

$$\mathcal{E} = B v \ell = (0.650)(3.40)(0.350) = 0.7735 \text{ V}$$

### Step 2: Induced Current

$$I_{ind} = \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R} = \frac{0.7735}{0.250} = 3.094 \text{ A}$$

### Step 3: Force on the Current-Carrying Wire in the Field

The induced current flows through the wire segment (length $\ell$) that is still inside the field. The magnetic force on this segment opposes the motion (Lenz's law):

$$F_{mag} = B I_{ind} \ell = (0.650)(3.094)(0.350) = 0.7039 \text{ N}$$

### Step 4: Required Applied Force

For constant velocity, the applied force equals the opposing magnetic force:

$$\boxed{F_{applied} = 0.704 \text{ N}}$$

### Alternative: Direct Power Method

$$F = \frac{\mathcal{E}^2}{Rv} = \frac{B^2\ell^2 v}{R} = \frac{(0.650)^2(0.350)^2(3.40)}{0.250}$$

$$= \frac{0.4225 \times 0.1225 \times 3.40}{0.250} = \frac{0.17588}{0.250} = 0.704 \text{ N} \checkmark$$

### Energy Analysis

**Power delivered by applied force** (= power dissipated in resistor):
$$P = Fv = 0.704 \times 3.40 = 2.39 \text{ W}$$

**Check via Joule heating**:
$$P = I_{ind}^2 R = (3.094)^2 \times 0.250 = 9.57 \times 0.250 = 2.39 \text{ W} \checkmark$$

---

## Key Concepts

1. **Motional EMF**: $\mathcal{E} = Bv\ell$ as a conductor moves through a magnetic field
2. **Lenz's Law**: the induced current always opposes the change in flux
3. **Braking force**: $F = B^2\ell^2 v/R$ — proportional to velocity (magnetic damping)
4. **Energy conservation**: work done by applied force = electrical energy dissipated
